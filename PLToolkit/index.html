The first 15 minutes of every job. Administrative rework. Incorrect paperwork, missing signatures, mis-coded time entries, and incomplete job documentation create a background hum of rework that individually seems trivial but collectively adds up to hours per week of non-productive time.

What Actually Walks Out The Door

The salary replacement cost — recruiting, hiring, training — is the visible expense. It is also the smaller part of the problem. The larger and less recoverable loss is what SHRM calls "role-specific institutional knowledge" — the accumulated judgment, relationships, workarounds, and pattern recognition that a veteran develops over years or decades on the job.

This is not information that exists in any manual. It includes:

Customer-specific knowledge. How to handle the customer who always changes specs mid-job. Which purchasing manager at your biggest account needs a phone call before you send a quote. The unwritten tolerances that Customer X actually cares about versus the ones on the print that are legacy holdovers. What "ASAP" means when Customer Y says it (Tuesday) versus when Customer Z says it (they forgot about it and Friday is fine).

Process judgment calls. The machine setting that is not in the manual but makes the difference between a clean run and scrap. The vendor substitution that works when the specified material is backordered. The sequence of operations that the veteran knows to reverse on a particular part geometry because the standard sequence causes chatter. The sound the machine makes when it is about to fail — not documented anywhere, but the veteran hears it and shuts down before damage occurs.

Emergency and exception handling. What to do when the fire suppression system trips a false alarm at 2 AM. Which supplier answers on weekends. The workaround for the ERP system when it rejects a valid entry. The physical trick to restart the compressor when it locks up in cold weather.

Relationship capital. The inspector who trusts the veteran's word on a marginal call. The subcontractor who prioritizes your shop because of a 15-year relationship with your foreman. The supplier rep who calls your veteran first when limited-availability material comes in.

When this person leaves, none of that walks out in a box. It just disappears. And the replacement — even a skilled, experienced hire — starts at zero on all of it. The 6–12 month ramp-up that SHRM documents is not about technical skill. It is about rebuilding this invisible infrastructure of judgment and relationships from scratch.

The Fix: 30-Day Pricing & Margin Reset

This is the second-fastest win in the entire report, behind only card/payment optimization. It requires no capital investment, no new hires, and no technology purchases. It requires a calculator, your job records, and the willingness to look honestly at your numbers.

Week 1: The 5-Job Autopsy

Objective: Establish your actual margin reality on recent completed work.

Actions:

  1. Pull the records for your 5 most recently completed jobs — ideally a mix of job types, customers, and sizes.
  2. For each job, calculate the true total cost using this formula:
    • Direct labor hours × fully loaded hourly rate (wage + benefits + payroll taxes — typically 1.3–1.5x the base wage)
    • Plus: materials at actual purchase cost (not estimated)
    • Plus: subcontractor costs if any
    • Plus: overhead allocation (a simple method: total annual overhead ÷ total annual labor hours = overhead rate per labor hour, applied to this job's hours)
  3. Compare true total cost to what you billed. Calculate the actual net margin percentage on each job: (Billed – True Cost) ÷ Billed × 100.
  4. Rank the 5 jobs from highest to lowest margin. The spread will likely surprise you. Most shop owners who do this exercise for the first time discover a 15–20 point gap between their best and worst margins — meaning some jobs are generating 25% margin while others are barely breaking even at 5–8%.

Expected output: A clear picture of where your margin actually is versus where you think it is. This is the foundation for every pricing decision that follows.

Week 2: Reprice or Drop

Objective: Correct the worst margin offenders immediately.

Actions:

  1. Any job type showing a net margin below 10% needs immediate attention. You have three options: (a) raise the price to bring margin above 15%, (b) reduce cost by changing process, material, or vendor, or (c) stop doing that type of work.
  2. For your existing customer base, prepare a cost-justified price increase letter — not an apology, but a professional communication that explains: input costs have risen X%, your pricing has been stable for Y period, and the new pricing reflects current market conditions while maintaining the quality and reliability they expect. Provide 30 days notice.
  3. For new quotes going forward, implement a margin floor — no job quoted below 15% net margin without owner approval. This single rule prevents the slow bleeding of accepting work at thin margins because the shop needs to stay busy.

Week 3–4: Build the Pricing Cadence

Objective: Prevent this leak from re-opening.

Actions:

  1. Set a quarterly pricing review on your calendar — a 60-minute session where you review input cost changes (materials, fuel, labor rates) against your current pricing and adjust as needed.
  2. Begin tracking actual job-level margins on every completed job. This does not require software — a spreadsheet with columns for job ID, billed amount, total cost, and margin percentage is sufficient. After 90 days, you will have enough data to identify your most and least profitable job types, customers, and work categories.
  3. Review your customer profitability ranking quarterly. Your best customers are not necessarily your largest — they are the ones generating the highest margin per hour of your crew's time. Your pricing, capacity allocation, and relationship investment should reflect this ranking.
